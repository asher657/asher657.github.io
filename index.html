Introduction

  Moral foundations theory is a social psychological theory intended to explain the origins of and variation in human moral reasoning on the basis of innate, modular foundations. It was first proposed by the psychologists Jonathan Haidt, Craig Joseph and Jesse Graham, building on the work of cultural anthropologist Richard Shweder; and subsequently developed by a diverse group of collaborators, and popularized in Haidt's book The Righteous Mind.

  According to this theory, differences in people's moral concerns can be described in five moral foundations:

  • Care: protecting and caring for others; opposite of Harm
  • Fairness: rendering justice based on agreed rules; opposite of Cheating
  • Loyalty: standing with a coalition to compete with other coalitions; opposite of Betrayal
  • Authority: submitting to authority and tradition; opposite of Subversion
  • Sanctity: abhorrence for disgusting things; opposite of Degredation

  The relevance of these five foundations to a person's view of what is right or wrong is determined by the Moral Foundations Questionnaire, 30 statements used to rate each of the five foundations. The subjects are told to rank each statement on a scale from 0 ("not at all relevant—this has nothing to do with my judgments of right and wrong") to 5 ("extremely relevant—this is one of the most important factors when I judge right and wrong").
